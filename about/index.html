Journey of a Physicist and Aspiring Developer

Posted by YK

On Sept 9, 2018

I studied physics at Reed College (class of ‘10) and started the PhD program at the University of Oregon in 2010. At the UofO I was in Prof. Eric Corwin’s lab where I studied the physics of granular materials.

What are granular materials?

Granular materials are exactly what they sound like – a large collection of macroscopic objects, like sand at the beach. One of the intriguing features of granular matter is its tendency to exhibit fluid-like and solid-like behavior, depending on ambient conditions However, unlike conventional liquids and gases, granular systems are not sensitive to ambient temperature - they are "athermal."

Granular systems also provide a sandbox (pun certainly intended) to study emergent behavior in many-bodied systems. We may know the properties of an isolated constituent member of a granular system, like a grain of sand, but it doesn't explain a lot of their collective behavior. Instances of these emergent phenomena in granular systems include the Brazil nut effect, the Janssen effect, pattern formation, etc.

Cool, but why should we study these systems?

Intellectual curiosity aside, granular matter is the superset of materials like powders, soil, minerals, and so on. In other words, a huge chunk of objects we either consume, interact with directly or depend on to fuel the economy happen to fall under the category of granular materials. So it’s in our best interest to understand the physics driving the dynamics of these systems. Further, they also provide a test-bed or model system to study other interesting problems like the glass transition (example). You can even get creative and exploit the properties of these materials to build things like robotic grippers (see link below).

Roughly speaking, my graduate research focused on two separate projects.

My first project was on the so-called Janssen effect, named after a German engineer who was active in the late 19th century. The easiest way to understand or explain this is by drawing a comparison to the well-known relationship between pressure and the filling height of fluid in a container – i.e. pressure ∝ filling height.

In granular systems, the relationship is very different. Turns out, if you’re measuring the pressure at the bottom of a tall bucket being filled with sand/pebbles/powder/rocks/tennis balls/wood chips/etc. the pressure will initially appear to increase with filling height. Nothing unexpected. However, beyond some characteristic height (largely determined by coefficient of friction, relative size of the bucket) the pressure will start plateauing. This is known as the Janssen effect and it’s made possible by the formation of contact force networks between particles which help divert the increasing load towards the bounding walls. For a cool visualization of force networks in granular systems, check out the link below!

For my first project I looked at the Janssen effect in 2D and slightly different external conditions. The study was an effort to create a granular system with tunable effective coefficient of friction. The big discovery was the ability to turn the pressure saturation on or off based on the container geometry. With the usual Janssen effect “turned on” we* also found a way to continuously and predictively change the screening length at which the saturation kicked in.

If you’re curious and want to learn more, head over to this write-up on the work. If that really piques your interest, feel free to download the paper from here (unfortunately, the journal article is behind a pay-wall).

My second project was also related to granular materials, but this time I was looking at a completely different phenomenon. In brief, the goal of the project was to build an experiment to observe shock fronts in flowing granular matter and derive a mathematical model to predict the shape of the interface. If you’re super curious, and want more details, download the paper from here; again, the journal article is behind a pay-wall.

After graduate school, I worked for about a year and a half at an engineering/consulting firm in Portland, OR where we worked with manufacturers and end-users of sonar and navigation systems. My responsibilities ranged from technical writing, technical support, data analysis, QA testing and some web development work. It was a great experience that gave me a taste of the world outside of academia and more importantly, made me realize that my passion was in coding. I kept gravitating towards projects that involved programming in some capacity and eventually concluded that I want to do it professionally. I suddenly found myself having to pivot in my career. And this brought me to the Tech Academy coding boot-camp!
